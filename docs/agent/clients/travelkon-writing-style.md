# TravelKon Writing Style

TravelKon blog copy should feel like professional travel-tech advice: practical, grounded in real trip moments, and commercially useful without sounding like a hard sell.

## Voice

- Write for Australian travellers planning overseas trips.
- Use clear, direct explanations and specific traveller scenarios.
- Sound like someone who has actually used a phone overseas: airport arrivals, ride-share pickup points, hotel check-in messages, map pins, translation apps, ferry tickets, train platforms, group chats, bank OTPs, and hotspot panic.
- Prefer helpful decision guidance over broad destination inspiration.
- Add light personality through concrete observation, not jokes or hype.
- Keep claims measured: say "popular tourist areas", "often", "may", and "check your plan details" where coverage, activation, hotspot, pricing, or inclusions can vary.
- Mention TravelKon naturally as the connectivity option, usually after answering the reader's question.
- Avoid AI tells: no repetitive "whether you're..." constructions, no generic "seamless experience", no inflated superlatives, and no paragraph that could apply to any destination after swapping the country name.

## Structure

- Open with the practical answer quickly.
- Use question-led H2s and comparison tables where the search intent involves a decision.
- Include short paragraphs, scannable bullets, and traveller-type recommendations.
- Weave destination detail into the body copy: named suburbs, airports, landmarks, transit systems, apps, and common travel decisions.
- Use FAQs to cover setup, compatibility, phone number, hotspot, airport purchase, and app-use questions.
- For Shopify blog body HTML, use only content-body elements: `h2`, `h3`, `p`, `a`, `strong`, `em`, `ul`, `ol`, `li`, `table`, `thead`, `tbody`, `tr`, `th`, `td`, and `br`.

## Internal Linking

- Place the first commercial link after the reader's problem is clearly framed, not in the first sentence.
- Use category links at decision points, product links after plan-fit caveats, and blog links as natural onward reading.
- Surround links with context that explains why the destination helps; avoid naked "check out our plans" link drops.
- Avoid forcing exact-match anchors repeatedly.
- Anchors should describe the reader's next action or decision, not the asset type. Prefer "current Indonesia eSIM inclusions" over "the Indonesia eSIM product page", and "walk through the eSIM activation process" over "activation guide".

## External Sources

- Use external links sparingly and only for non-competing support.
- Preferred external links are official airports, customs/regulators, government tourism boards, public transport authorities, official attraction pages, and device/platform support pages.
- Do not link to competing eSIM/SIM sellers or affiliate SIM comparison sites in final article body copy.
- Competitor pages can inform structure and gaps, but keep them out of the published body unless explicitly approved.
- Never expose source-handling language to readers. Avoid phrases like "non-TravelKon reference", "external source", "official source", "this guide is a useful reference", or anything that sounds like internal editorial justification.
- Since the article is on the TravelKon blog, avoid third-person self-references such as "TravelKon's guide" unless the sentence genuinely needs the brand name. Prefer direct phrasing such as "the Bali packing guide" or "compare Singapore data eSIM options".

## Claim Rules

- Do not promise universal coverage, instant activation in every case, guaranteed speed, cheapest pricing, or all-device compatibility.
- Do not invent product inclusions, prices, expiry, hotspot support, network support, or activation timing.
- If a feature depends on the plan or device, state that it depends on the selected plan and the traveller's unlocked, eSIM-compatible phone.

## Product Recommendations

- Check the live TravelKon category and product pages before naming a product in article copy.
- Recommend products by fit: destination-only, multi-country route, physical SIM need, eSIM-compatible phone, data allowance, validity, hotspot needs, and calls/SMS needs.
- Treat "unlimited" as a product-detail term, not a blanket promise. Mention fair-use, roaming caps, reduced-speed data, or country-specific limits where relevant.
- Do not make out-of-stock products the main recommendation.
- Avoid price-led copy unless the brief specifically asks for price comparisons.
