# TravelKon Singapore Tourist SIM vs eSIM Improvement Plan

## Current Assessment

The article is valid and materially better than the first pass, but it still reads more like a polished SEO comparison than a confident professional travel-tech article. The structure is useful, the links are controlled, and the core recommendation is clear. The next improvement should focus on editorial rhythm, more lived Singapore scenarios, clearer product-commercial moments, and less repeated explanation of the same SIM/eSIM trade-off.

## Main Issues

### 1. The opening is good, but the article does not keep that same energy

The first paragraph has a real scene: red-eye arrival, Changi, Jewel, Grab, MRT. After that, the copy becomes more functional and starts repeating "choose eSIM if data; choose SIM if local number". The reader gets the point early, so later sections need to move into sharper examples, not restate the same decision.

### 2. Internal links are safer now, but still too clustered around direct comparison moments

The first two commercial links are natural enough, but they appear as a neat pair. That can still feel like SEO placement. The Singapore eSIM link should be tied to a more specific traveller problem, such as landing late and needing data before leaving Changi. The physical SIM link should sit where local calls/SMS and counter setup are being discussed.

### 3. The network section needs more practical judgement

The Singtel/M1/StarHub section names providers but does not add enough useful distinction. It should not make unsupported coverage claims, but it can explain what travellers should compare: pickup location, eSIM vs physical SIM availability, data-only vs calls/SMS, ID requirements, roaming add-ons, and validity period.

### 4. The Changi section can be more specific without overclaiming

The copy mentions counters and terminals, but it could better reflect actual arrival behaviour: after immigration, baggage, e-arrival forms, taxi/Grab pickup, MRT ticketing, and hotel check-in timing. This would make the Changi section feel written by someone who understands Singapore arrivals.

### 5. The comparison table is accurate but bland

The table is useful, but rows such as "Setup timing" and "Physical card" are generic. A stronger table would use decision-led rows:

- Arriving late at Changi
- Need a Singapore number
- Travelling with kids
- Business traveller with OTPs
- Stopover under 24 hours
- Continuing to Malaysia or another country

### 6. The article needs one stronger mini-framework

Right now it has sections and advice, but no memorable editorial device. Add a simple framework such as:

**The Singapore Connectivity Test**

- Do you need a local phone number?
- Do you need data the moment you leave Changi?
- Is your phone unlocked and eSIM-compatible?
- Will your Australian number need to receive SMS?
- Are you staying in Singapore only, or continuing elsewhere?

This would make the article more useful and less AI-like.

### 7. The final CTA is too soft

There is no confident final commercial close before FAQs. Add a brief recommendation paragraph after the traveller-type section that says:

- If you mainly need data, sort the eSIM before flying.
- If calls/SMS matter, choose a tourist SIM.
- If unsure, check device compatibility first.

Then link naturally to the Singapore eSIM category or physical SIM category, depending on the recommendation.

## Improvement Plan

### Phase 1: Rework the article flow

Keep the same approved headings, but change what each section does:

- Intro: Keep the Changi arrival scene, tighten to 2 paragraphs.
- Quick Answer: Add "The Singapore Connectivity Test" as bullets.
- Changi section: Make it more arrival-specific and move the physical SIM link here.
- Provider section: Add a practical comparison checklist rather than more generic provider commentary.
- eSIM section: Move the Singapore eSIM category link into a specific "before you fly" sentence.
- Comparison table: Change from feature-led to traveller-scenario-led.
- Traveller type section: Add short, punchy recommendations for leisure, business, family, and stopover travellers.
- FAQs: Keep concise; remove repeated wording already covered above.

### Phase 2: Improve anchors and placements

Recommended placements:

| Destination | Natural anchor | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Singapore eSIM category | `sort your Singapore data before you fly` | In the eSIM setup section, after explaining install/activation timing. |
| Singapore SIM category | `compare physical Singapore SIM options` | In the Changi/physical SIM section, after explaining local number/calls/SMS needs. |
| Singapore activation blog | `check the activation steps while you still have WiFi` | In the setup section, not as a generic guide link. |
| Plug checklist blog | `make sure every device can actually charge` | In family/device packing context. |
| Perth guide | `plan the Perth-to-Singapore arrival details` | FAQ or final planning note only if it does not distract. |
| Changi external source | `Changi's SIM card purchase guide` | Keep only once in the airport section. |

### Phase 3: Add more Singapore-specific phone-use scenes

Weave in scenarios like:

- Finding the right Grab pickup zone at Changi.
- Checking MRT routes from Changi to City Hall, Orchard, or Chinatown.
- Opening attraction tickets at Gardens by the Bay or Sentosa.
- Messaging a hotel before check-in.
- Coordinating dinner plans around Marina Bay, Tiong Bahru, Chinatown, or Lau Pa Sat.
- Receiving bank OTPs while using an overseas data plan.
- Keeping kids entertained or devices charged during a family day out.

### Phase 4: Remove remaining generic phrasing

Watch for:

- "The smart move is..."
- "The upside is obvious..."
- "Neither option wins every trip..."
- "The phone is logistics HQ..."

These are acceptable once, but the piece should lean more on specific examples than polished generic advice.

### Phase 5: Validation

After rewrite:

- Run `scripts/validate_blog_html_copy.py`.
- Confirm all internal and external links return 200.
- Confirm no competitor/eSIM seller links are in the final body.
- Confirm the primary keyword appears naturally near the opening.
- Confirm there are at least 5 Singapore-specific phone-use scenarios.

## Recommended Rewrite Direction

Do not make the article longer for the sake of it. Aim for roughly 1,250-1,450 words, but make the extra length come from sharper examples, a stronger decision framework, and better link placement rather than more explanation.
